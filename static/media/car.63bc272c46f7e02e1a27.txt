/section Project Overview
As part of the Advanced Mechatronics course at UT Austin, I worked on a team to design and manufacture a high-performance RC car optimized for circuit racing.
The design focused on achieving a balance between modularity, mechanical robustness, and power-to-weight ratio to ensure both speed and track reliability.

/section Chassis Engineering and Structural Validation
I led the structural design and material optimization of the vehicle frame, focusing on a lightweight modular architecture.
/bullet
Iterative Material Selection: While the initial design utilized ABS, manufacturing constraints required a pivot to PLA. To maintain structural integrity while reducing mass, I optimized the chassis thickness from 6.35 mm to 5.3 mm, using geometry to regain the stiffness lost by the thinner profile.
FEA Validation: I conducted stress, displacement, and strain analyses within SolidWorks to ensure the chassis could withstand drivetrain torque and high-speed impacts during race conditions.
Serviceability: I designed a modular frame featuring custom cap fittings, allowing for rapid battery replacement and track-side maintenance.
/endbullet

/section Steering Kinematics and Stability
The primary challenge was achieving cornering stability within a compact footprint.
/bullet
Single-Axle Design: I implemented a simplified steering system using high-infill PLA, providing a balance of precision and give to prevent catastrophic failures of the servo motor during collisions.
Modularity: I maintained a small chassis footprint that allowed easy access to the steering linkage and servo for quick adjustments between race heats.
/endbullet

/section Drivetrain and RPM/Torque Analysis
While I did not design the physical gearbox, I was responsible for part of the mechanical validation of the powertrain components to ensure performance reliability under load.
/bullet
RPM and Torque Analysis: I performed calculations on gear mesh forces and rotational inertia to ensure the Rigid White Resin gears could sustain the high RPMs and torque spikes required for competitive circuit starts.
Material Selection: I validated the use of a carbon fiber rear axle to provide maximum torsional stiffness with minimal rotational inertia, ensuring efficient power transfer to the wheels.
/endbullet

/section Functional Body Kit and Protection
Inspired by Formula 1 aesthetics, I modeled a custom 3D-printed body kit including front and rear wings and side skirts.
/bullet
Sacrificial Armor: While the profiles were aerodynamic, the primary function of the front wing was to serve as a structural impact guard. It was engineered as a crumple zone to protect the steering linkages and servo from direct wall collisions.
Electronic Integration: The side skirts and chassis panels provided secure mounting points for internal electronics, keeping the center of gravity low while shielding the battery and wiring from debris.
/endbullet

/section Performance Review and Failure Analysis
/table
Component | Performance Result | Engineering Takeaway
Chassis | High durability; the thickness reduction to 5.3 mm did not compromise the frame. | FEA was an accurate predictor of real-world structural needs.
Protection | The front wing successfully absorbed three major impacts, saving the steering rack. | Functional armor is more critical than high-speed aerodynamics for indoor circuit racing.
Drivetrain | Failure point: under extended loading, gear teeth began to shear and set screws backed out. | Resin gears lacked the fatigue limit for high-acceleration shock loads. Future iterations require metal gears and thread-locking compound.
/endtable

/section Root Cause Analysis of Drivetrain Failure
Post-race inspection revealed that while the gears performed well in initial tests, they failed under sustained racing conditions.
/bullet
Stress Concentration: Sudden acceleration and deceleration applied peak forces to individual gear teeth, exceeding the material's shear strength and leading to tooth breakage.
Vibration and Fastener Fatigue: High-frequency vibrations caused the motor pinion set screws to back out, leading to misalignment and further gear damage.
Impact Loading: Wall impacts transmitted kinetic energy through the drivetrain, causing shock loads that the 3D-printed resin could not dissipate.
/endbullet
