/section Reinforcement Learning for Thin-Object Manipulation

/subsection Project Overview
This research investigates using reinforcement learning (RL) to enable a dexterous three-fingered hand to perform human-like manipulation of thin objects, such as playing cards and coins.
By leveraging the sensorized fingernails of the custom PLATO Hand, we compared different control action spaces to determine which produced the most stable and robust grasps in contact-rich environments.

/image /plato_rl/rl_enviornment.png aspect=16:9, crop=center
/caption RL simulation environment used for parallelized policy training.

/subsection The Challenge: The "Thin-Object" Problem
Traditional robotic grippers struggle with thin objects because they often rely on soft fingertips that lack precision and high mechanical impedance.
Humans solve this by using rigid fingernails to provide tactile feedback and stable interfaces for tasks like picking up a single coin.

/image /plato_rl/card_grasping.png aspect=16:9, crop=center
/caption Card grasping setup highlighting thin-object contact.

Goal: Use model-free RL to discover grasp strategies that utilize fingernail edges to lift objects off flat surfaces.

Complexity: The search space for dexterous manipulation is massive, requiring highly efficient simulation and precise reward shaping.

/subsection Technical Approach
/image /plato_rl/RL_plan.png aspect=16:9, crop=center
/caption RL training plan and staged progression from basic lifting to thin-object manipulation.

1. Simulation and Physics Modeling

/bullet
Parallelized training: Utilized IsaacLab to train across 4,096 environments simultaneously.
Massive data scale: Generated nearly 900 million samples, equivalent to 205 real-world days of training, in just 2.5 hours on a workstation RTX 4090 GPU.
Transmission modeling: Because URDFs cannot natively simulate the PLATO Hand's closed-loop 5-bar linkage, we implemented a transmission model to convert it to an open-chained revolute-revolute finger for the physics engine.
/endbullet

2. Reward Engineering and Curriculum Learning

/bullet
Dynamic weighting: We started with lower penalties for joint velocity and acceleration to encourage exploration, increasing them by an order of magnitude after 20% of training.
Fine-grained rewards: We utilized a palm contact penalty to force the policy to use the fingers and fingernails for finer manipulation.
Goal tracking: Orientation tracking rewards were phased in only after the policy learned basic lifting to prevent reward hacking the goal pose.
/endbullet

/subsection Key Results: Ball Grasping with Torque Control
In step 3 of the training plan, torque control produced stable spherical-object grasping with smoother contact transitions than position control.
The learned policy maintained force-closure while reducing jitter during lift and hold phases.

/subsection Key Results: Card Grasping with Fingernail Lift
In step 4, the policy successfully executed fingernail-assisted card lifting by sliding under the edge and establishing a stable contact interface.
This behavior demonstrates the intended thin-object strategy enabled by rigid fingernails and reward curriculum shaping.

/subsection Key Results: Torque vs. Position Control
We conducted quantitative payload tests to compare how each action space handled increasing mass for a playing card.

/table
Metric | Joint-Position Control | Direct Torque Control
Stability | Jittery; produces high-frequency vibrations | Smooth; fine-grained force-closure
Payload Capacity | Fails at 0.09kg | Success up to 0.15kg (+67%)
Grasp Quality | Limited to form-closure | Achieves stable force-closure
/endtable
